Airline Revenue Management 101

I work in Revenue Management at Air Canada, and I get asked fairly often what that actually means. The honest answer is that it's a mix of economics, statistics, and a bit of psychology — all applied to the problem of filling an airplane as profitably as possible. This page is my attempt to explain the key ideas, from the intuitions to some of the actual math, without making it feel like a textbook. The field has a beautiful internal logic to it once you see how each piece builds on the last.

Chapter 00 — Why Revenue Management?

The whole thing starts with one uncomfortable fact: when a plane takes off with an empty seat, that seat is gone forever. You can't put it back on the shelf. You can't sell it the next day. It just evaporates. And since the plane was going to fly anyway — the fuel is burned, the crew is paid — that empty seat is pure lost revenue with essentially zero cost saving attached to it.

That's what makes airlines special. Most businesses can adjust their supply when demand changes. Airlines mostly can't, at least not in the short run. The plane has a fixed number of seats, and every pricing decision on one seat is implicitly a decision about every other seat on the same flight.

Add to that the fact that not all passengers are equal. The person booking six months out for a beach holiday and the consultant booking the morning of their client meeting are buying the exact same seat — but their willingness to pay can differ by a factor of four or five. Revenue Management exists to serve both without letting one steal the other's price point.

The core tension
Sell too cheap too early and you fill the plane with low-fare passengers, leaving no room for the business traveler who'd have paid 4× more. Hold prices too high for too long and you depart with empty rows. Every RM decision navigates that tension, flight by flight, day by day.

Academic studies generally estimate that good RM adds somewhere between 4 and 8% to airline revenues compared to naive pricing. For a major carrier, that's hundreds of millions of dollars a year — not from flying more planes, but from pricing the existing ones better.

Chapter 01 — Fare Classes & the Bucket System

Before any forecasting or optimization can happen, airlines need a structure to work within. That structure is the fare class — a letter (Y, K, Q, V, and so on) that bundles a price with a set of rules: refundability, changeability, advance purchase requirements. These restrictions exist for one purpose: to make passengers sort themselves into the segment that actually matches their price sensitivity.

ClassTypical rulesWho buys itYield
Y / JFully refundable, no advance purchaseBusiness, corporateHigh
K / MChange fee, partial refundSemi-flexible travelerMid
Q / V / LNon-refundable, advance purchase requiredLeisure, price-sensitiveLow

The idea is that a business traveler could technically buy the non-refundable advance-purchase fare — but they won't, because it doesn't work for how they travel. The restrictions act as a self-selection mechanism. One classic example: for decades, discount fares required a Saturday night stay. Business travelers almost never want to spend a weekend away from home, so that single rule cleanly separated two segments without any airline employee making a judgment call on each booking.

One thing worth clarifying: booking class (the letter) and cabin class (Economy, Business) are different things. Two passengers sitting next to each other in Economy can hold Q and Y fares and have paid very different prices under very different conditions. The letter is invisible to the passenger but central to everything the RM system does.

Chapter 02 — Class-Based Forecasting

Inventory control is only as good as the forecast feeding it. Before the system can decide which fare classes to open or close, it needs to predict what demand looks like — for each fare class, on each flight, at each point in the booking window. This is class-based forecasting, and it's the backbone of traditional RM.

Booking curves

The most fundamental concept here is the booking curve — a picture of how reservations accumulate over time before departure. Every route has its own characteristic shape. Long-haul leisure routes see a burst of early bookings, a lull, then a spike close to departure. Short-haul business routes are flatter, with most bookings arriving in the last two weeks. The RM system continuously compares actual bookings against the historical curve: ahead of pace means demand is strong; behind means you may need to open cheaper fares. It's the system's real-time pulse check on each flight.

The unconstraining problem

Here's a subtle but important challenge. If a cheap fare class was closed 45 days before departure, you never observed the demand that existed at that price. Your historical data only shows what you actually sold — not what was there. Feed that biased data back into a forecasting model and you'll systematically underestimate true demand at low fares. Unconstraining is the statistical process of inferring what demand would have been, had the bucket been open. It's not the most glamorous part of RM, but getting it wrong can quietly distort every downstream decision.

Pickup forecasting

A common practical approach is pickup forecasting: rather than modelling demand from scratch, you look at how many bookings historically arrive in a given future time window given today's booking position. If a flight currently has 80 bookings with 30 days to go, and historically those flights pick up 40 more in the final 30 days, you forecast 120 total. Simple, interpretable, and widely used — though it struggles when demand patterns shift significantly from historical norms, which happens more often than you'd like.

The limit of buckets
Class-based forecasting assumes demand naturally organizes itself into discrete fare buckets. In reality, passenger willingness to pay is a continuous spectrum. The bucket structure is an administrative convenience — not an economic truth. That tension is exactly what Chapter 04 addresses.

Chapter 03 — Leg Inventory Control: Littlewood, EMSR-a & EMSR-b

With a forecast in hand, the system needs to act on it. How many seats should be protected for each fare class? This is the inventory control problem, and it has a beautiful mathematical lineage that starts in 1972 with a surprisingly simple idea.

Littlewood's rule — the two-class foundation

Ken Littlewood was the first to formally solve the airline seat allocation problem, for the simple case of two fare classes. His insight: you should accept a low-fare booking request if and only if its fare exceeds the expected revenue from keeping that seat available for a future high-fare passenger. Formally, denote the low fare as $f_1$ and the high fare as $f_2$, with $f_1 < f_2$. Let $D_2$ be the (random) future demand for the high fare class, and $C$ the remaining capacity. Littlewood's rule says: accept the low-fare request if and only if:

Littlewood's Rule (1972)
$$f_1 \geq f_2 \cdot P(D_2 \geq C)$$
where $P(D_2 \geq C)$ is the probability that future high-fare demand will meet or exceed remaining capacity $C$.

The right-hand side is the expected marginal revenue of keeping the seat: the high fare, discounted by the probability that a high-fare passenger actually materializes to fill it. If the low fare exceeds this, take it. If not, wait. Elegant, and it works — but only for two fare classes. Real flights have many more.

EMSR-a — extending to multiple classes

Belobaba (1987) generalized Littlewood's rule to $n$ fare classes through the Expected Marginal Seat Revenue algorithm. In EMSR-a, you compute protection levels for each fare class independently by applying Littlewood's two-class logic pairwise. For each lower class $j$ being considered, you protect seats for each higher class $i > j$ separately and sum those protections:

EMSR-a protection level for class $j$
$$S_j^a = \sum_{i > j} y_i^*$$
where each $y_i^*$ solves $f_j = f_i \cdot P(D_i \geq y_i^*)$, i.e. the Littlewood threshold applied to each higher class $i$ against the current lower fare $f_j$.

EMSR-a is intuitive and easy to implement, but it has a known flaw: treating each higher class independently tends to over-protect when fares are close together, because the same capacity ends up being "claimed" by multiple higher classes.

EMSR-b — the industry standard

EMSR-b, also from Belobaba (1992), fixes this by aggregating all higher classes into a single composite class before applying Littlewood's rule. You compute a weighted average fare $\bar{f}$ across all higher classes and a combined demand distribution $\bar{D}$, then find the protection level $S_j^b$ that satisfies:

EMSR-b protection level for class $j$
$$f_j = \bar{f} \cdot P(\bar{D} \geq S_j^b)$$
where $\bar{f} = \dfrac{\sum_{i > j} f_i \cdot E[D_i]}{\sum_{i > j} E[D_i]}$ is the demand-weighted average fare of all higher classes, and $\bar{D} = \sum_{i > j} D_i$ is their aggregated demand.

The key difference from EMSR-a: you're asking "how many seats should I protect for all higher classes combined, given the aggregated demand and average fare?" rather than class by class. This avoids double-counting and produces better protection levels when fares are close together — which in practice they often are. EMSR-b is the dominant approach in commercial RM systems today.

Working through the logic numerically
You have one seat left. A discount passenger offers $180. Your forecast says there's a 55% chance a high-fare passenger books before departure at $480. Expected marginal revenue of holding: $480 × 0.55 = $264 > $180. Hold the seat — the bid price is $264.

Two hours later — same seat, same offer. Departure is 4 hours away and the high-fare probability has dropped to 25%. Expected marginal revenue: $480 × 0.25 = $120 < $180. The bid price just fell below the offered fare. Sell.

Chapter 04 — Class-Free Forecasting & Willingness to Pay

Class-based forecasting works, but it carries a hidden assumption baked in from the start: that demand naturally organizes itself into discrete fare buckets. In reality, passenger willingness to pay is a continuous spectrum — every passenger has their own threshold, and none of them know or care about your fare class ladder. Class-free forecasting lets go of that assumption entirely.

What willingness to pay actually is

Willingness to pay (WTP) is the maximum price a given customer would accept before choosing not to buy. It's not a fixed property of a person — it varies with context, urgency, alternatives, and even mood. A passenger booking a spontaneous weekend trip has a different WTP than the same passenger booking the same route for a funeral. WTP is better thought of as a distribution than a number.

Formally, if we denote a passenger's WTP as the random variable $W$, then the probability that a passenger buys at price $p$ is simply:

Purchase probability at price $p$
$$P(\text{buy} \mid p) = P(W \geq p) = 1 - F_W(p)$$
where $F_W(p)$ is the CDF of the WTP distribution — the fraction of the population with WTP below $p$. The higher the price, the fewer passengers buy.

This is the demand curve, expressed in terms of individual WTP. And the revenue-maximizing price is the one that balances the gain from charging more per ticket against the loss from fewer passengers buying. This trade-off is captured in the expected revenue function:

Expected revenue at price $p$ (single unit)
$$R(p) = p \cdot P(W \geq p) = p \cdot [1 - F_W(p)]$$
The optimal price $p^*$ maximizes $R(p)$. Taking the derivative and setting it to zero gives: $1 - F_W(p^*) - p^* \cdot f_W(p^*) = 0$, where $f_W$ is the PDF of the WTP distribution.

Why the shape of the WTP distribution matters enormously

Different WTP distributions lead to very different optimal pricing strategies, and this is something I find genuinely interesting to think about. If the WTP distribution is concentrated — most passengers have similar valuations — you want a fairly uniform price, because price discrimination doesn't add much value. If it's dispersed, with a long tail of high-WTP passengers, price discrimination (i.e. fare classes, or dynamic pricing) becomes extremely valuable. The wider the distribution, the more money is left on the table by a single price.

A simple illustration
Suppose your flight has 3 passengers with WTP of $100, $200, and $500. If you charge a single price of $200, you earn $400 (two passengers buy). If you charge $100, you earn $300. But if you can price-discriminate perfectly — charging each passenger exactly their WTP — you earn $800. The gap between $400 and $800 is the value of perfect segmentation. Real fare class systems try to capture some of that gap without having to read each passenger's mind.

Estimating WTP in practice

The key challenge is that you never directly observe WTP — you only observe whether a passenger booked at a given price. Someone who books at $250 has WTP ≥ $250, but you don't know how much more they'd have paid. This is a classic censored observation problem. Parametric approaches fit a distribution (log-normal and Weibull are common choices, as they enforce positivity and produce a plausible right tail) to the observed booking data. The log-normal is particularly convenient: it assumes $\ln(W) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, which gives:

Log-normal WTP model
$$F_W(p) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{\ln p - \mu}{\sigma}\right)$$
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF, $\mu$ is the mean of log-WTP, and $\sigma$ its standard deviation. The parameters $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are estimated from historical booking data using maximum likelihood.

The problem with a single parametric distribution is that real markets have multiple customer segments — leisure travelers with low, concentrated WTP and business travelers with high, dispersed WTP. A single log-normal misses this. Mixture models address it by summing two or more distributions with mixing weights:

Mixture WTP model (two segments)
$$F_W(p) = \lambda \cdot F_{W_1}(p) + (1 - \lambda) \cdot F_{W_2}(p)$$
where $\lambda \in (0,1)$ is the proportion of leisure passengers, and $F_{W_1}$, $F_{W_2}$ are the WTP CDFs for leisure and business segments respectively. The parameters are typically estimated via EM (Expectation-Maximization).

From WTP distributions to pricing

Once you have a WTP distribution, the EMSR logic generalizes naturally. The bid price at any point in the booking window is simply the price $p$ such that the expected marginal revenue of the next seat equals the cost of giving it up — and that calculation now uses the continuous WTP distribution instead of discrete fare class probabilities. This is conceptually what class-free systems do: they replace the ladder of buckets with a continuous demand curve, and the protection levels with a continuously-updated bid price derived from it.

The buy-down problem

Class-based systems also have a hard time with buy-down — when passengers who would have paid a higher fare instead book a cheaper one that's been left open. A class-free model that estimates the full WTP distribution handles this naturally: it knows that opening a $150 fare will pull some demand away from the $280 fare, and it prices that cannibalization into the optimization. Class-based forecasting, by contrast, records a booking at $150 and has no way of knowing it displaced a $280 booking that never materialized.

Chapter 05 — Network Optimization

Everything so far treats each flight leg as an independent problem. But airlines operate networks — a passenger flying Montréal to Frankfurt to Istanbul uses two legs, and optimizing each one in isolation ignores the relationship between them. Network optimization is where classical RM gets genuinely hard, and also genuinely interesting.

Why leg-based RM fails on networks

A simple example. Your Montréal–Frankfurt leg has one seat left. Two requests arrive simultaneously: a local passenger paying $420 for that leg only, and a connecting passenger paying $850 total for Montréal–Frankfurt–Istanbul, with $360 attributed to the first leg. A leg-based system sees $420 vs. $360 and accepts the local passenger. The network just left $430 on the table. This isn't an edge case — it's a systematic bias. Leg-based RM always undervalues connecting passengers because it can't see beyond its own leg.

Network bid prices and the LP formulation

The solution is to compute bid prices at the network level. The standard approach formulates a linear program over all flights and all itineraries simultaneously. Let $x_{ij}$ be the number of passengers accepted on itinerary $j$ using leg $i$, $f_j$ the fare for itinerary $j$, and $C_i$ the capacity of leg $i$. The network LP maximizes total revenue subject to capacity constraints on every leg:

Network Revenue Management LP
$$\max \sum_j f_j \cdot x_j$$ $$\text{subject to: } \sum_{j \ni i} x_j \leq C_i \quad \forall \text{ legs } i$$ $$x_j \leq E[D_j] \quad \forall \text{ itineraries } j, \quad x_j \geq 0$$
The dual variables $\pi_i$ on the capacity constraints are the network bid prices — the shadow price of one unit of capacity on leg $i$.

An itinerary $j$ should be accepted if and only if its fare exceeds the sum of bid prices across all legs it uses:

Network acceptance criterion
$$f_j \geq \sum_{i \in j} \pi_i$$
Displacement cost
The sum $\sum_{i \in j} \pi_i$ is the displacement cost of itinerary $j$ — the opportunity cost of the capacity it consumes across the network. A connecting itinerary using two congested legs has a high displacement cost. One on two lightly-loaded legs has a low one. This single criterion captures both the value of the itinerary and the true cost of accepting it.

Virtual nesting

Solving this LP in real time for every incoming request is computationally intensive. Virtual nesting is a practical approximation: compute the network bid price offline, use it to calculate each itinerary's net contribution (fare minus displacement cost), rank itineraries by that contribution, and map them to virtual booking classes. The leg-based system then controls availability using these virtual classes — approximating network-optimal decisions without re-solving the full LP each time.

When displacement cost really bites
Imagine Frankfurt is congested on a summer Saturday — every flight near-full, bid prices high. A Montréal–Frankfurt–Mumbai itinerary priced at $1,100 sounds attractive. But if the FRA–BOM leg carries a bid price of $600 and YUL–FRA carries $380, the displacement cost is $980. Net contribution: $120. It might rationally get declined in favor of two local bookings each generating more net value.

Chapter 06 — Dynamic Pricing

Classical RM — with its fare classes, buckets, and EMSR-b — is fundamentally a system of availability control. Prices are set by pricing teams and filed in advance; RM decides which pre-set prices to show. Dynamic pricing breaks that assumption. Instead of choosing from a fixed menu, the system continuously computes an optimal price based on current conditions. The price itself becomes the decision variable.

The continuous pricing problem

In classical RM, a fare class is either open or closed — binary. Dynamic pricing replaces this with a continuous price function. At any moment, with $c$ seats remaining and $t$ time until departure, the optimal price $p^*(c, t)$ solves:

Dynamic pricing optimality condition
$$p^*(c,t) = \text{argmax}_p \; \left[ p \cdot \lambda(p) \cdot \Delta t + V(c, t) \right]$$
where $\lambda(p)$ is the demand rate at price $p$ (bookings per unit time), $\Delta t$ is a small time interval, and $V(c, t)$ is the value function — the expected total revenue from $c$ remaining seats and $t$ time to departure. This is a stochastic dynamic programming formulation. The key insight: the optimal price equates the marginal revenue of selling now against the change in value from having one fewer seat.

In practice, this is typically solved via approximate dynamic programming or simulation, since the exact DP is computationally intractable for full airline networks. But the intuition is clean: price high when $c$ is low (scarcity) or when $t$ is small (late demand tends to be inelastic), price lower when you have plenty of capacity and plenty of time.

Why now?

Dynamic pricing as a concept is as old as supply and demand. What changed is the ability to execute it at scale — cloud infrastructure, NDC distribution, and ML-based demand estimation have made continuous pricing operationally feasible in the 2020s in ways it simply wasn't a decade ago.

The anchoring problem

One underappreciated challenge is customer psychology. If a fare was $300 yesterday and is $480 today, some passengers feel deceived — even if both prices are rational. Research in behavioral economics shows that price anchoring and perceived fairness materially affect purchase decisions. Airlines have to balance revenue optimization against the risk of alienating customers who find the pricing arbitrary. It's a real tension, and one reason continuous dynamic pricing hasn't fully replaced the more legible fare class structure even where it's technically possible.

Chapter 07 — Offer & Order

Traditional RM optimizes one thing: the seat. But a seat is not the only thing an airline sells. Bags, seat selection, lounge access, meals, Wi-Fi, partner products — ancillary revenue now represents anywhere from 15% to over 50% of total revenue depending on the carrier. The next frontier of RM is optimizing not just a seat price, but an entire offer.

The legacy distribution problem

The airline industry's distribution infrastructure was built in the 1960s around the Passenger Name Record — a rigid structure designed for a world where airlines sold exactly one product through a small number of channels. IATA's New Distribution Capability (NDC) standard replaces this with a flexible API-based architecture capable of representing any product, any bundle, any personalized offer constructed dynamically rather than pulled from a pre-filed fare catalog.

What NDC actually enables
Instead of "here is the Q fare with these rules," an airline using NDC can respond to a search with a fully assembled, personalized offer: seat + bag + priority boarding + fare, priced for this customer in this context in real time. The offer is built on the fly; the price reflects the customer's full value, not just the seat component.

Attribute-based selling and bundle optimization

Attribute-based selling deconstructs the product into its components — window seat, extra legroom, flexibility, meal — and prices each separately. RM's role shifts from seat inventory control to optimizing a combinatorial space of bundles. The optimization objective changes accordingly: it's no longer "maximize seat revenue" but "maximize total passenger revenue," and a passenger paying a low seat fare with high ancillary spend might be more valuable than a high-fare passenger who buys nothing else. Connecting loyalty data, booking systems, and real-time offer engines to actually compute this is still mostly a work in progress at most carriers.

Chapter 08 — What's Next

Revenue Management has come a long way from EMSR-b running overnight batch jobs in the 1980s. The pace of change is accelerating, and the version of RM practiced in 2035 will probably look as different from today's as today's looks from where it started. Here are the directions I find most interesting.

Reinforcement learning

Classical RM is model-driven: it assumes demand follows a statistical distribution and optimizes within that model. Reinforcement learning takes a different approach — learn a pricing policy directly from interacting with the market, without assuming any particular demand structure. An RL agent sets prices, observes outcomes, and updates its policy to improve over time. What I find genuinely appealing about RL is that it naturally handles the sequential nature of RM decisions — the prices you set today affect the demand you observe tomorrow, which affects your prices the day after. Classical methods treat these decisions largely as independent. RL doesn't, and in volatile markets that distinction matters a lot.

Real-time everything

Classical RM systems run on batch cycles — forecasts and optimizations computed overnight, controls updated a few times a day. The direction of travel is continuous, real-time optimization responding to bookings, competitor moves, and external signals as they happen. Most legacy airline systems weren't built for this, and the transition is happening, but slowly. Airline IT tends to move at its own pace.

Search data and richer demand signals

Booked fares tell you what passengers paid. Search data tells you what they considered. The gap between a search and a booking — who searched, what they were shown, what they chose, what they abandoned — is an extraordinarily rich signal for WTP estimation that classical RM largely ignores. As airlines gain more control over their distribution through NDC and direct channels, this data becomes available, and the forecasting models that can use it become dramatically more precise. The ideal, long-term, is a WTP distribution estimated from individual-level behavioral signals in near-real-time. We're not there yet, but it's not science fiction either.

RM beyond aviation

The methods developed in airline RM have already spread to hotels, car rentals, cruises, and rail. The next wave is likely to reach industries with historically static pricing: restaurants, parking, sports venues, healthcare. The core intuitions — perishability, fixed capacity, demand segmentation, opportunity cost — are universal. What changes is the domain.
