Turkish From Zero — A Complete Grammar Guide

I started learning Turkish in November 2022, and it's been one of the most rewarding and humbling experiences I've had with a language. Turkish is genuinely different from anything a French or English speaker has encountered. Not harder exactly — just built on completely different principles. Once those principles click, the language becomes surprisingly logical, even beautiful in its regularity. This page is my attempt to lay everything out clearly, from the very first sounds to the suffixes that mark fluency.

Chapter 00 — Why Turkish is Different

Before you learn a single word, it helps to understand the architecture of the language you're entering. Turkish belongs to the Turkic language family, completely unrelated to Indo-European languages like French, English, or even Persian. It's an agglutinative language — meaning it builds words by stacking suffixes onto a root, one after another, each suffix adding a precise grammatical meaning.

In French you might say je ne pourrai pas y aller — six words. In Turkish: gidemeyeceğim — one word. Every piece of that word is a suffix with its own rule. That's the game.

Three principles that govern everything
1. Agglutination: suffixes stack onto roots in a fixed order.
2. Vowel harmony: every suffix adjusts its vowels to match the root.
3. SOV word order: Subject → Object → Verb. The verb always comes last.

Turkish also has no grammatical gender — no masculine/feminine distinction anywhere. There is no equivalent of le/la, un/une, der/die/das. There are no articles at all. And the verb to be is a suffix, not a standalone word. None of this is hard once you accept that you're learning a different system entirely, not a variation of what you already know.

Chapter 01 — Alphabet & Pronunciation

Turkish uses a Latin alphabet introduced by Atatürk in 1928, replacing the Ottoman Arabic script. It has 29 letters. The key principle: Turkish spelling is almost perfectly phonetic. Every letter has exactly one sound. If you can read it, you can pronounce it correctly — which is a luxury you don't have in French or English.

The two letters that will trip you up
I ı (dotless i) — a sound that doesn't exist in French or English. The back of your tongue goes up and back, lips are unrounded. Think of the sound you make when hesitating: "uh". It's not "ee". Confusing ı and i changes word meanings completely.

Ğ ğ (soft g) — never pronounced as a consonant. Between two vowels it slightly lengthens the preceding vowel. At the end of a word, almost silent. Dağ sounds like "daa", öğrenci like "öö-ren-ci".

Consonant mutation — voiced and voiceless

Turkish has a systematic rule: certain final consonants change when a vowel-initial suffix is added. The voiceless consonants ç, k, p, t soften to c, ğ, b, d when followed by a vowel. This is called consonant mutation and you will see it constantly.

Consonant mutation rule
ç → c  |  k → ğ  |  p → b  |  t → d

ağaç (tree) → ağacı (the tree / accusative)
kitap (book) → kitabı (the book / accusative)
sebep (reason) → sebebi
renk (color) → rengi

Chapter 02 — Vowel Harmony

Vowel harmony is the single most important rule in Turkish. Everything else builds on it. The principle is simple: the vowels in a word must come from the same family. And when you add a suffix, the vowel in that suffix must match the last vowel of the root.

Turkish has 8 vowels, split into two groups based on where in the mouth they're produced:

GroupVowelsPosition
Back vowelsa, ı, o, uProduced at the back of the mouth
Front vowelse, i, ö, üProduced at the front of the mouth

If a word's last vowel is a back vowel, all suffixes use back vowels. If it's a front vowel, all suffixes use front vowels. This applies to every suffix, stacked one after another throughout a long word.

The 2-way harmony (e/a)

Some suffixes only vary between e and a. The rule: after a front vowel → e, after a back vowel → a.

2-way harmony: suffix vowel = e or a
Front vowels (e, i, ö, ü) → suffix uses e
Back vowels (a, ı, o, u) → suffix uses a

ev (house) → evde (at home — locative)
araba (car) → arabada (in the car — locative)
gelmek (to come) → gelmek
gitmek (to go) → gitmek

The 4-way harmony (i/ı/ü/u)

Other suffixes vary among four vowels. The rule depends on both frontness and roundedness:

Last vowel of rootSuffix vowelExample
a, ııkız → kızı (the girl)
e, iiev → evi (the house)
o, uutop → topu (the ball)
ö, üügöz → gözü (the eye)
The harmony mnemonic
Think of it as a mouth position: your tongue stays in roughly the same place throughout a word. If the root pulls your tongue back (a, ı, o, u), the suffix vowels stay back. If the root pulls it forward (e, i, ö, ü), the suffixes follow forward. Vowel harmony is phonological muscle memory — your mouth is being consistent.

Exceptions — loanwords

Loanwords from Arabic, Persian, French, or English often violate vowel harmony within the root itself (e.g. kitap — ki is front, a is back). In these cases, the suffix follows the last vowel only, ignoring internal disharmony. So kitap → kitabı (back, because last vowel is a).

Chapter 03 — Nouns, Plurals & the Absence of Articles

Turkish has no articles. There is no word for a, an, the, un, une, le, or la. The noun stands alone. Ev can mean "a house", "the house", or just "house" depending on context. You'll miss articles for about two weeks and then wonder why you ever needed them.

Plurals — ler / lar

The plural suffix follows 2-way vowel harmony: -ler after front vowels, -lar after back vowels.

Plural suffix
Front vowel root → root + ler
Back vowel root → root + lar

ev → evler (houses)
araba → arabalar (cars)
kitap → kitaplar (books)
gün → günler (days)
No plural after numbers
In Turkish, nouns after numbers stay singular. Üç araba = "three car" (not "three cars"). The number already tells you it's plural — adding -lar would be redundant. This trips up learners constantly.

Indefiniteness: the numeral bir

The closest thing to the article "a/an" is bir (one), placed before the noun. Bir araba = a car. But you'll often omit it entirely in natural speech when the context makes indefiniteness clear.

Possession suffixes (iyelik ekleri)

Possession is expressed by a suffix on the noun, not a separate word. These follow 4-way vowel harmony:

PersonSuffix (after consonant)Example (ev = house)Meaning
1st sg.-im / -ım / -um / -ümevimmy house
2nd sg.-in / -ın / -un / -ünevinyour house
3rd sg.-i / -ı / -u / -üevihis/her/its house
1st pl.-imiz / -ımız / -umuz / -ümüzevimizour house
2nd pl.-iniz / -ınız / -unuz / -ünüzevinizyour (pl.) house
3rd pl.-leri / -larıevleritheir house

When the noun ends in a vowel, a buffer consonant s is inserted before the 3rd person suffix: araba → arabası (its car). For 1st and 2nd person after a vowel, the suffix loses its first vowel: araba → arabam (my car).

The verb to be — a suffix, not a word

In the present tense, "to be" doesn't exist as a separate verb. It's a suffix added directly to a noun or adjective. The suffix follows 4-way vowel harmony and changes based on person:

Present "to be" suffixes (-(y)im, -sin, ∅, -iz, -siniz, -ler)
Ben öğrenciyim — I am a student
Sen öğrencisin — You are a student
O öğrenci(dir) — He/She is a student (dir often omitted)
Biz öğrenciyiz — We are students
Siz öğrencisiniz — You (pl.) are students
Onlar öğrenciler — They are students

After a consonant-final noun, you add the suffix directly. After a vowel-final noun, a buffer y is inserted: araba → arabayım (I am a car — not very useful, but grammatically correct).

Negative to be — değil

Değil means "not" and is placed before the to-be suffix: öğrenci değilim (I am not a student). Değil itself takes the to-be suffixes.

Past to be — -di/-ti suffix on noun

The past of "to be" adds -di after the noun with the appropriate personal ending: öğrenciydim (I was a student), öğrenciydin (you were a student), öğrenciydi (he/she was).

Chapter 05 — The Six Noun Cases

Turkish marks the grammatical role of nouns with case suffixes. There are six cases. Once you have these, you can build almost any sentence structure. Think of them as answering specific questions about a noun's role.

CaseSuffixAnswersExampleMeaning
Nominative—Who/What (subject)Adam geldiThe man came
Accusative-(y)i/ı/u/üWho/What (definite object)Adamı gördümI saw the man
Dative-(y)e/aTo where / To whomEve gidiyorumI'm going home
Locative-de/da/te/taWhere (static)Evde oturuyorumI'm sitting at home
Ablative-den/dan/ten/tanFrom where / From whomEvden geliyorumI'm coming from home
Genitive-(n)in/ın/un/ünWhose / Of whatEvin kapısıThe door of the house
Accusative — only for definite objects
This is a subtlety that surprises many learners. The accusative suffix only marks a specific, definite direct object. If the object is indefinite ("I ate a sandwich"), no accusative. If it's definite ("I ate the sandwich"), accusative is required.

Sandviç yedim — I ate a sandwich (indefinite — no suffix)
Sandviçi yedim — I ate the sandwich (definite — accusative suffix)

Locative vs. ablative after voiceless consonants

When the noun ends in a voiceless consonant (ç, f, h, k, p, s, ş, t), the locative becomes -te/ta and the ablative becomes -ten/tan instead of -de/da and -den/dan. This is consonant assimilation — the d hardens to match the voiceless environment.

Accusative: evi
Dative: eve
Locative: evde
Ablative: evden
Genitive: evin
Case suffix full conjugation — araba (car, back vowel, ends in vowel)
Nominative: araba
Accusative: arabayı (buffer y)
Dative: arabaya (buffer y)

Ablative: arabadan

Genitive + possession = the Turkish noun phrase

Turkish expresses possession differently from English. The possessor takes the genitive suffix; the possessed takes the possession suffix. Together they form a noun phrase: Ahmet'in arabası = Ahmet's car (lit. "of Ahmet, his car").

Telling time

Turkish time is a bit quirky. The hours go on the locative (at) or accusative (to), and "past" vs "to" use different constructions:

  • Saat üç — It's 3 o'clock
  • Saat üçü on geçiyor — It's 10 past 3 (lit. "it passes 3 by ten")
  • Saat dörde on var — It's 10 to 4 (lit. "ten exists to four")
  • Saat üçbuçuk — It's half past 3 (buçuk = half)

Chapter 07 — Adjectives & Word Order

Adjectives never change

Unlike in French or German, Turkish adjectives have no agreement with the noun they modify. Güzel (beautiful) stays güzel regardless of number, case, or anything else. This is a genuine relief. Adjectives always precede the noun they modify: güzel ev (beautiful house), büyük arabalar (big cars).

Predicative adjectives

When the adjective is the predicate ("the house is beautiful"), it takes the to-be suffixes just like nouns: Ev güzel or more emphatically Ev güzeldir. In first/second person: Ben mutluyum (I am happy — mutlu + yum).

Comparison

  • Comparative: daha + adjective — daha büyük (bigger)
  • Superlative: en + adjective — en büyük (biggest)
  • "Than" uses the ablative case: ondan daha büyük (bigger than him)

Word order — SOV and the principle of left-branching

Turkish is strictly SOV: Subject → Object → Verb. The verb always comes at the end of the clause. But the deeper principle is left-branching: everything that modifies something else comes before what it modifies. Relative clauses come before the noun they modify. Adjectives come before nouns. Postpositions come after nouns. Time expressions typically come before place expressions.

Word order comparison
English: I bought a red bag at the market yesterday.
Turkish: Ben dün pazarda kırmızı bir çanta satın aldım.
(I yesterday market-at red a bag bought.)

Notice: time (dün) → place (pazarda) → adjective (kırmızı) → object (çanta) → verb (last).

Chapter 08 — Ability & Possibility: -ebilmek

The ability suffix -ebil- / -abil- inserts between the verb stem and the tense suffix. It expresses both "can" (ability) and "might" (possibility) depending on context and tense. It's extremely high-frequency — you'll need it almost immediately.

Ability suffix formation
Verb stem ending in consonant: stem + ebil / abil + tense + person
Verb stem ending in vowel: stem + bil + tense + person

gel- (come) → gelebiliyor (is able to come / can come)
git- (go) → gitebilirim (I can go)
yap- (do) → yapabilirim (I can do it)
ye- (eat) → yebilirim (I can eat)

Inability — -eme- / -ama-

The negative of ability is formed with -eme- / -ama- (the bil drops completely in the negative):

Inability formation
gel- → gelemeyorum (I cannot come — present)
git- → gitemem (I can't go — aorist negative)
yap- → yapamam (I can't do it)
gel- → gelemedim (I couldn't come — past)

Possibility vs. ability

The same suffix covers both. Yağmur yağabilir = "It might rain" (possibility). Türkçe konuşabiliyorum = "I can speak Turkish" (ability). Context distinguishes them, usually cleanly.

How do you say "I couldn't sleep" in Turkish? (uyu- = to sleep, -di past tense)

Chapter 09 — Present Continuous: -iyor

The present continuous is the first tense most learners acquire, and for good reason — it's regular, predictable, and covers a huge amount of everyday speech. It describes actions happening right now or actions in progress over a period.

Present continuous formation
Verb stem + iyor/ıyor/uyor/üyor + personal suffix

The -iyor part does NOT harmonize in the usual 4-way pattern — it always uses the rounded vowel that matches the last vowel of the stem:
After e, i → iyor
After a, ı → ıyor
After o, u → uyor
After ö, ü → üyor

Stem changes before -iyor

If the verb stem ends in a vowel, that vowel is dropped before -iyor: ye- (eat) → yiyor (not ye-iyor). de- (say) → diyor. This is a notable irregularity.

Chapter 10 — The Aorist: -ir / -er / -ar / -ur

The aorist (geniş zaman — "wide time") is one of Turkish's trickiest tenses for learners because its suffix is irregular for many verbs. It expresses habitual actions, general truths, and permanent states — roughly the English simple present ("I read books every day", "water boils at 100°C").

Regular aorist formation

For verbs with stems of two or more syllables: add -ir/-ır/-ur/-ür (4-way harmony). For verbs with one-syllable stems ending in a vowel: add -r directly.

Aorist suffix — multisyllabic stems
gel- (come) → gelir (he/she comes habitually)
git- (go) → giter — !! irregular (one of 13 exceptions)
bekle- (wait) → bekler (vowel-final → just r)
oku- (read) → okur

The 13 irregular aorist verbs

Thirteen common one-syllable verbs take -er/-ar instead of -ir/-ır. These must be memorized: almak, bilmek, bulmak, durmak, gelmek, görmek, kalmak, olmak, sanmak, ölmek, vermek, varmak, vurmak.

Negative aorist — a complete irregularity

The negative aorist is entirely different: stem + -mez/-maz (not -mir/-mır). Gelmez (he/she doesn't come habitually). First person singular is even more irregular: gelmem (I don't come — not *gelmezim).

Chapter 11 — Past Witnessed: -di

Turkish has two distinct past tenses, and the distinction between them is one of the most fascinating features of the language. The -di past (geçmiş zaman) is used when you personally witnessed or directly experienced the event. It's the default past tense for things you saw, did, or were present for.

-di past formation (4-way harmony + consonant assimilation)
After voiced sounds: stem + di/dı/du/dü + personal suffix

gel- → geldim (I came — witnessed)
git- → gittim (I went — voiceless t)
oku- → okudum (I read)
iç- → içtim (I drank — voiceless ç)

Chapter 12 — Past Reported: -miş & Evidentiality

The -miş past is what makes Turkish genuinely special. It marks events that you did not personally witness — things you heard about, read about, inferred, or discovered after the fact. Linguists call this evidentiality: grammatically encoding how you know what you're claiming. Very few languages outside of Turkish and its relatives grammaticalize this distinction so cleanly.

-miş past formation (4-way harmony)
stem + miş/mış/muş/müş + personal suffix

gel- → gelmişim (I apparently came / I've come — reported)
git- → gitmiş (he apparently went)
uyu- → uyumuş (she apparently slept)
düş- → düşmüş (it apparently fell)
Persongelmek — -miş past
Bengelmişim
Sengelmişsin
Ogelmiş
Bizgelmişiz
Sizgelmişsiniz
Onlargelmişler
The three registers of -miş
1. Reported speech: Ahmet gelmiş — "Ahmet came (apparently / I heard)"
2. Discovery: Anahtarımı kaybetmişim! — "I've lost my keys!" (just discovered)
3. Sarcasm / storytelling: Used in fairy tales (bir varmış bir yokmuş = "once upon a time") and for ironic distance from what someone just said.

The evidential distinction in practice

Consider: your friend tells you they went to Paris. You tell someone else. In Turkish you must use -miş: Paris'e gitmiş. If you say Paris'e gitti (-di past), you're claiming you were there and witnessed it. Getting this wrong doesn't just sound odd — it communicates something false about your epistemic position. It matters.

You wake up and notice your keys are gone. You say to yourself "I lost my keys!" — which past tense?

Chapter 15 — Negation & Questions

Verbal negation — -me / -ma

The negation suffix -me/-ma is inserted between the verb stem and the tense suffix. It follows 2-way harmony. The key thing to remember: the position of -me is always the same, but some tenses produce contractions when combined with it.

Negation position
Verb stem + me/ma + tense suffix + personal suffix

gel- + me + iyor → gelmiyor (not coming) — vowel contracts
git- + me + ecek → gitmeyecek (won't go)
yap- + ma + dı → yapmadı (didn't do)
gel- + me + r → gelmez (doesn't come — aorist special)
The -miyor contraction
In the present continuous negative, -me + iyor contracts: the e of -me merges with the i of -iyor, giving -miyor. So gelmiyor not *"gelme-iyor". This contraction is predictable once you've seen it a few times.

Question particle — mi / mı / mu / mü

Questions are formed with the particle mi/mı/mu/mü, which follows 4-way vowel harmony and is always written as a separate word. It inserts after the verb but before the personal suffix in most tenses, or at the very end in some constructions.

Question formation
Statement: Geliyorsun — You are coming
Question: Geliyor musun? — Are you coming?

Statement: Türkçe konuşuyor — He speaks Turkish
Question: Türkçe konuşuyor mu? — Does he speak Turkish?

Statement: İstanbul'a gideceksin — You will go to Istanbul
Question: İstanbul'a gidecek misin? — Will you go to Istanbul?

Chapter 17 — Compound Verbs with etmek, olmak & yapmak

A huge proportion of Turkish vocabulary consists of compound verbs: a noun or adjective borrowed from Arabic, Persian, or French, paired with one of three light verbs. These light verbs — etmek (to do/make), olmak (to be/become), yapmak (to do/make) — carry the grammatical load while the nominal root carries the meaning.

etmek — the most common light verb

Etmek is used with Arabic- and Persian-derived nouns. The noun stays unchanged; etmek conjugates normally. Key point: in spoken Turkish the stem of etmek irregularly uses ed- in some forms.

Common etmek compounds
telefon etmek — to phone
yardım etmek — to help
kabul etmek — to accept
devam etmek — to continue
tercih etmek — to prefer
özür dilemek (not etmek) — to apologize

olmak — to be / to become

Olmak is both a standalone verb ("to become", "to happen", "to be okay") and a light verb. As a light verb it expresses a state being reached: mutlu olmak (to be/become happy), hazır olmak (to be ready).

Var and yok — existence, not olmak
"There is" and "there isn't" in Turkish use var and yok, not olmak. Para var = there is money. Süt yok = there is no milk. These are standalone words, not verbs, and they're extremely high-frequency.

yapmak — to do / make

Yapmak tends to combine with action nouns, especially in colloquial speech: alışveriş yapmak (to go shopping), spor yapmak (to do sport), hata yapmak (to make a mistake).

Chapter 19 — Imperatives & Requests

The imperative

The Turkish imperative is the bare verb stem for informal singular commands. For formal or plural, add -(y)in/-(y)ın/-(y)un/-(y)ün. For very formal/official, add -(y)iniz.

Imperative forms
Informal singular: bare stem — Gel! (Come!), Git! (Go!)
Formal / plural: stem + in/ın/un/ün — Gelin! Gidin!
Very formal: stem + iniz/ınız/unuz/ünüz — Geliniz! Gidiniz!
Negative: stem + me/ma + imperative — Gelme! (Don't come!)

Softening requests — -e bilir misin? and -er misin?

A bare imperative is direct and can sound rude. For polite requests Turkish typically uses the question form of the aorist or future: Bana yardım eder misin? (Can/will you help me?), Gelebilir misin? (Can you come?).

The let's form — -elim/-alım

To say "let's do something", add -elim/-alım to the stem: Gidelim (Let's go), Yiyelim (Let's eat), Çalışalım (Let's work).

Chapter 20 — Relative Clauses & Verbal Nouns

This is where Turkish becomes genuinely complex for Indo-European speakers. Because Turkish is left-branching, relative clauses come before the noun they modify — the opposite of English and French. And they're formed by converting the verb into a participial form.

Verbal nouns (mastar and -me/-ma)

The infinitive suffix -mek/-mak forms verbal nouns (like English "to go", "going"). More commonly in subordinate clauses, the verbal noun suffix -me/-ma is used with possession suffixes to make gerund-like constructions:

Verbal noun with possession
gelmem — my coming
gitmen — your going
okuması — his/her reading

Benim gelmemi istedi — He wanted me to come
(lit. "He wanted my coming")

Relative clauses — -(y)en/-(y)an and -(d)ik

Turkish has two main participial suffixes used in relative clauses. -(y)en/-(y)an is used when the modified noun is the subject of the relative clause. -(d)ik (with possession suffix) is used when the modified noun is the object or other role:

Participial relative clauses
The man who comes: gelen adam (gel + en + adam)
The woman who went: giden kadın (git + en → giden)

The book that I read: benim okuduğum kitap (oku + duk + um + kitap)
The city that she lives in: onun oturduğu şehir
Why this feels backward
In English: "the book [that I read]" — noun first, clause follows.
In Turkish: "[benim okuduğum] kitap" — clause first, noun at the end.
The entire relative clause folds into what feels like an adjective placed before the noun. This is consistent with Turkish's left-branching principle but takes real adjustment to produce naturally.

Chapter 21 — Causative, Passive & Reciprocal

Turkish can modify the basic meaning of a verb by inserting derivational suffixes between the stem and the tense suffix. These are productive — they work on almost any verb.

Causative — -dir / -tir / -ir / -t / -it

The causative suffix means "to make/cause someone to do something" or "to have something done". The form varies depending on the stem:

Causative suffix
After most consonants: stem + dir/dır/dur/dür
After voiceless consonants / some verbs: + tir/tır/tur/tür
Short stems (1 syllable ending in vowel): + t

giy- (wear) → giydirmek (to dress someone)
ye- (eat) → yedirmek (to feed someone)

Passive — -il / -ıl / -ul / -ül or -in / -n

The passive suffix converts an active verb to passive ("it was done", "it is seen"):

Chapter 23 — Diminutives & Expressive Suffixes

Turkish has a rich system of expressive suffixes that add emotional color, affection, or nuance to words. You'll hear them constantly in spoken Turkish, especially in informal registers.

The diminutive — -cik / -cığ / -çık

The suffix -cik/-cığ/-çık/-cük (4-way harmony, with consonant assimilation) adds smallness, cuteness, or affection. It's used with nouns, adjectives, and even adverbs:

Diminutive suffix -cik / -çık
küçük (small) → küçücük (tiny, sweet-small)
az (little) → azcık (a tiny bit)
ev (house) → evcik (little house, cozy house)
kız (girl) → kızcağız (poor little girl — with compassion)
bir (one) → bircik (just one / only one)

The -cağız / -ceğiz suffix — sympathy and pity

This suffix conveys compassion or pity toward the noun: adamcağız (poor man), çocukcağız (poor little child). It's warm and humanizing, not condescending.

The -ca / -ce suffix — languages and "in the manner of"

Used to form language names and adjectives meaning "in the manner of": Türk → Türkçe (Turkish language), Fransız → Fransızca (French), çocuk → çocukça (childish / in a childish manner).

Reduplication for emphasis

Turkish uses reduplication (repeating words) for intensification, especially with adjectives and adverbs: yavaş yavaş (slowly, very slowly), ağır ağır (heavily, slowly), çabuk çabuk (quickly, hurriedly). It has a rhythmic, conversational feel.

Chapter 25 — Pitfalls for French & English Speakers

After a few years of learning Turkish, I've collected a list of the mistakes I make reliably. Most come from instincts built by French and English that don't transfer.

1. Forgetting vowel harmony under pressure

When you're thinking fast, the wrong suffix vowel comes out. The most common error: using -de when you need -da, or -im when you need -ım. The fix is to build the habit of mentally checking the last vowel of the root before every suffix. It becomes automatic eventually.

2. Putting the verb in the wrong place

French and English speakers instinctively place verbs in the middle of sentences. In Turkish the verb is always last. When sentences get long and complex, the urge to place the verb earlier gets stronger. Resist it. Even a ten-clause sentence ends with the verb.

3. Confusing -di and -miş

This is genuinely hard because the distinction doesn't exist in French or English. The rule of thumb: if you were there and saw it, use -di. If you read it, heard it, inferred it, or discovered it after the fact, use -miş. When in doubt about your own actions, use -di (you were presumably present for your own life).

4. Adding plural after numbers

Üç kitaplar is wrong. Always üç kitap. This error is so deeply ingrained from European languages that it takes months to eliminate.

5. Using the accusative on indefinite objects

Bir kitabı okudum sounds strange — the accusative implies definiteness, but bir kitap is indefinite. Say bir kitap okudum. The accusative is only for specific, identifiable objects.

6. Translating prepositions directly

French sur, sous, dans, avant don't map one-to-one to Turkish postpositions. Turkish often uses case suffixes where French would use a preposition: "in Istanbul" = İstanbul'da (locative case — no postposition needed). The urge to find the Turkish equivalent of every French preposition leads to using postpositions where a bare case suffix is correct.

7. Expecting grammatical gender everywhere

There is none. O is he, she, and it. Onun is his, her, and its. You never need to specify gender unless you want to for clarity. This should be a relief, but French speakers keep looking for a gender system that isn't there.
